**COMMON FORMS**
» Tablets and capsules: The most common forms for oral ingestion.
» Extended-release formulations: Designed to provide a sustained effect throughout the day.
» Liquid solutions: For those who have difficulty swallowing pills.

**COMMON WAYS TAKEN**
» Oral ingestion: Pills or liquids taken by mouth.
» Snorting: Some users crush the pills to snort the powder for a faster, more intense effect.
» Injection: Less common, involves dissolving the powder in water and injecting it.

**POSSIBLE SHORT-TERM HEALTH EFFECTS**
» Psychological effects: Increased alertness, attention, and energy; euphoria.
» Physical effects: Increased heart rate and blood pressure, reduced appetite, insomnia, and increased breathing rate.
» Dangerous effects: High doses can lead to dangerously high body temperature, irregular heartbeat, and cardiovascular failure.

**POSSIBLE LONG-TERM HEALTH EFFECTS**
» Psychological issues: Paranoia, hostility, and even psychosis.
» Physical health issues: Cardiovascular problems and malnutrition due to reduced appetite.

**OTHER HEALTH-RELATED ISSUES**
» Infectious diseases: Increased risk of HIV, hepatitis B and C, and other blood-borne viruses from needle sharing.

**WITHDRAWAL SYMPTOMS**
» Physical symptoms: Fatigue, increased appetite, and hypersomnia or insomnia.
» Psychological symptoms: Depression, irritability, and sleep disturbances.

**TREATMENT OPTIONS WITH MEDICATION**
Currently, there are no FDA-approved medications specifically designed to treat addiction to prescription stimulants.

**CENTRAL NERVOUS SYSTEM DEPRESSANTS**
**Commercial Names** - Barbiturates: pentobarbital (Nembutal); Benzodiazepines: alprazolam (Xanax), chlordiazepoxide (Librium), diazepam (Valium), lorazepam (Ativan), triazolam (Halicon); Sleep Medications: eszopiclone (Lunesta), zaleplon (Sonata), zolpidem (Ambien).
